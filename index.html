    Navigating Success: Choosing the Right College Major

    This project involves a thorough analysis of data concerning recent graduates in the United States, focusing on their employment outcomes and choice of majors. The examination encompasses the distribution of graduates into full-time, part-time, and contract positions, alongside identifying majors associated with higher earning potential for female graduates. Additionally, the study investigates gender demographics across various academic disciplines to delineate fields dominated by either women or men.

    Key Points:
    1. High-Paying Majors: Engineering and technology majors consistently offer higher earning potential compared to other fields of study.

    Conclusion:
    In conclusion, choosing the right college major for success involves careful consideration of various factors. While fields like engineering and technology offer higher earning potential, it's essential to recognize and address gender disparities in certain majors. Making informed decisions based on data analysis can help individuals better position themselves for success in their careers and financial futures.

By understanding the reasons behind employee departures, organizations can develop targeted strategies to improve employee satisfaction and retention. These findings underscore the importance of addressing employee concerns and implementing effective retention measures to foster a positive work environment.

    Comparison of Dimensionality Reduction Techniques: PCA VS t-SNE

    In this project, our objective is to visualize high-dimensional data obtained from Kaggle's Digit Recognizer competition, consisting of images represented as 784-dimensional vectors. To achieve this, we utilize dimensionality reduction techniques such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and t-SNE (t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding). PCA identifies the directions of maximum variance in the data and projects it onto a lower-dimensional space, while t-SNE focuses on preserving the local structure of the data. Our implementation includes steps such as data preprocessing, manual PCA for 2D visualization, employing Scikit-Learn's PCA, and applying t-SNE for further dimensionality reduction and visualization. Through this project, we aim to gain insights into the underlying structure of the dataset and evaluate the effectiveness of different dimensionality reduction methods in visualizing complex data patterns.

    Conclusion: PCA provided limited separation of data, while t-SNE exhibited superior performance in preserving local structure and separating data points effectively.

    The project aims to compare PCA and t-SNE for visualizing high-dimensional data, providing insights into their effectiveness in dimensionality reduction and visualization.

    Project Overview: Predicting Tumor Type

    This project focuses on predicting whether a tumor is benign or malignant using machine learning techniques. It utilizes the Breast Cancer Wisconsin (Original) dataset, consisting of 569 instances with 30 numeric attributes and a target class.

    Implementation Steps:

    1. Data Preparation: The dataset is loaded and checked for missing values. 2. Visualization: Pair plots are created to visually compare features for malignant and benign tumors. 3. Model Training: Support Vector Machine (SVM) algorithm is employed for classification. 4. Model Evaluation: The SVM model achieves an accuracy of 96.49%.

    Conclusion: The SVM model demonstrates strong predictive performance, with a 96.49% accuracy, indicating its effectiveness in distinguishing between benign and malignant tumors. This project showcases the use of machine learning for medical diagnosis, specifically in predicting tumor type based on relevant features extracted from medical imaging data.
